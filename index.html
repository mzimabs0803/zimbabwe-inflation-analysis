Executive Summary: The Economic Lifecycle

Phase 1: The Multi-Currency Era (2010–2016)

Following the collapse of the original Zimbabwean dollar in 2008, the nation adopted a multi-currency system dominated by the US Dollar. This resulted in immediate stabilization, with inflation dropping to 3.02% by 2010. By 2015, the country experienced deflation (-2.4%), a rare economic state caused by a severe lack of liquidity and the strengthening of the USD against regional currencies.

Phase 2: Bond Notes & Rising Volatility (2017–2018)

To solve liquidity shortages, the [Reserve Bank of Zimbabwe](https://www.rbz.co.zw/) introduced "Bond Notes" pegged to the USD. However, a lack of trust led to a thriving black market exchange rate. By the 2018 General Election, inflation began to creep back into double digits as the government increased domestic borrowing to fund agricultural programs.

Phase 3: The RTGS Collapse (2019–2023)

In 2019, the government officially ended the multi-currency era, re-introducing a local currency (the RTGS/Zimdollar). The market reacted violently; according to [Macrotrends data](https://www.macrotrends.net/global-metrics/countries/ZWE/zimbabwe/inflation-rate-cpi), inflation spiked to 255% in 2019 and hit an annual peak of 557% in 2020. This period was characterized by "blended inflation" tracking, as citizens relied on the informal market for survival.

Phase 4: The Gold Anchor & ZiG (2024–2026)

The introduction of the gold-backed **ZiG (Zimbabwe Gold)** in April 2024 marks the most significant policy shift in a decade. Unlike previous attempts, the ZiG is supported by 2.5 tonnes of gold and foreign currency reserves. Projections from the [IMF](https://www.imf.org/en/Countries/ZWE) suggest a stabilization to 12% by late 2026, provided the central bank refrains from printing money to fund deficits.
