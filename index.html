Analysis: The Decadal Economic Cycle

1. Stability & Deflation (2010–2016)

Following the hyperinflation crisis of 2008, Zimbabwe adopted a multi-currency system. This move anchored the economy, leading to a period of remarkable stability where inflation remained in the single digits. By 2015, the economy actually entered a deflationary phase (-2.4%), driven by a strengthening US Dollar and a critical shortage of liquidity in the domestic market.

2. Bond Notes & Transition (2017–2018)

To address the US Dollar shortage, the government introduced "Bond Notes." While officially pegged 1:1 with the USD, market confidence quickly eroded. This led to the emergence of a parallel market and the beginning of a new inflationary climb as the nation prepared for leadership transitions in 2017 and the 2018 General Election.

3. The Currency Crisis (2019–2023)

The formal re-introduction of a local currency (RTGS) in 2019 triggered a massive market shock. Inflation skyrocketed to **557% by 2020**. Despite a temporary dip in 2021 as the USD was re-authorized for use, the lack of a stable commodity anchor resulted in chronic price instability for nearly five years.

4. Gold Backing & The ZiG (2024–Present)

In April 2024, the Reserve Bank launched the **ZiG (Zimbabwe Gold)**. Unlike previous local units, the ZiG is supported by 2.5 tonnes of physical gold and $100 million in foreign reserves. According to **[IMF Reports](https://www.imf.org/en/Countries/ZWE)**, this shift is expected to lower inflation to 12% by late 2026, marking a new chapter in Zimbabwe's monetary history.
